# Hypokalemia

## Causes

- Low potassium intake
- Excessive GI loss
  - Vomiting, diarrhea, fistula, ileostomy
- Excessive renal loss
  - Hyperaldosteronism
  - Cushing's syndrome
  - Renal tubular acidosis
  - Magnesium deficiency
  - Diuretics
- Redistribution into cells
  - Insulin
  - Beta-agonists
  - Alkalosis
  - Hypokalemic periodic paralysis

## Features

- Weakness, hypotonia
- Depression
- Ileus, constipation
- Ventricular arrhythmias - Torsades de pointes
- Coma
- Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus when chronic
- **ECG changes**
  - Prolonged PR
  - Inverted T waves
  - U waves

## Management

> 1 mmol drop on RFT = 200 mmol total body potassium deficit

- Oral dosages
  - PO Syrup KCl
    - 1 gram = 13.5 mmoles
  - PO slow K
    - 1 tablet = 600 mg = 8 mmoles
  - PO potassium citrate
    - 1 ml = 1 mmole
  - PO phosphate-sandoz
    - 1 tablet = 3 mmoles K + 16 mmoles PO4
- Premixed IV
  - 500 ml NS + 10 mmoles K
  - 500 ml NS + 20 mmoles K
  - 500 ml D5 + 10 mmoles K
  - 500 ml D5 + 20 mmoles K
  - 500 ml Hartmann comes with 2.5 mmoles K

### Severe hypokalemia

> \<2.5 mmol/L or ECG changes

- Consult ICU and attach cardiac monitor
- KCl 20 mmol/hour mixed with saline
  - Up to 80-100 mmol/L concentration
  - Use central line for high concentration KCl
- Oral 3-4 gram syrup KCl q4h
  - Each dose contains 30-40 mmoles

### Moderate hypokalemia

> \>2.5 mmol/L and normal ECG

- Oral 2-3 gram syrup KCl q4h for 2-3 doses
  - Each dose contains 20-30 mmoles
- KCl 10-20 mmol/hour mixed with saline
  - Up to 40 mmol/L
  - Continuous IV infusion
  - **Never administer KCl bolus**
